Ratio of Black to White Maternal Mortality:

Looking back on the past 100 years, Black parents have always had a greater chance of dying a pregancy-related death. For over 80 years, that chance has increased and remained at a 2 times likelihood and has continued to increase on average. Today, Black parents face a 86.7% greater risk of pregancy-related deaths than their white counterparts, a key statistic in America’s Black maternal health crisis.

Source: https://www.commonwealthfund.org/publications/issue-brief-report/2020/dec/maternal-mortality-united-states-primer
https://www.cdc.gov/reproductivehealth/maternal-mortality/pregnancy-mortality-surveillance-system.htm

Maternal Mortality Ratio by Race/Ethnicity (2016-2018):

National pregnancy-related mortality survillence can help us get a clearer understanding of the demographics, circumstances, and situations that elevate a parent’s chance of dying a pregnancy-related death. Conducted by the CDC, their 2016-2018 survillance showed that white parents face the smallest risk of maternal mortality: for every 100,000 births, 13.7 white maternal deaths occur. On the other hand, there are 41.4 Black maternal deaths for the same number of births.

Source: https://www.cdc.gov/reproductivehealth/maternal-mortality/pregnancy-mortality-surveillance-system.htm

Maternal Mortality Rate by Level of Education and Race/Ethnicity:

You may think that a higher level of education ensures access to a higher quality of healthcare — yet that only seems to be the case for white mothers. In fact, white mothers with less than a high school education still have a reduced maternal mortality rate compared to Black mothers with a college degree or higher. This data can illuminate the fact that health and quality healthcare is not a matter of success, but a matter of systemic racism embedded deep into its roots.

Source: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31487273/
